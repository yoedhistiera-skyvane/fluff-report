Executive Summary

Two things changed in early June, close together, which makes it easy to blame the wrong one. Stripe Checkout went live on 1 June. Four days later, on 5 June, we began consolidating: we stopped running the old FluffCo3 ad account and moved everything into the FluffCo account. Here is the order of events:

Since the move, blended ROAS has fallen from 1.84 to 1.27 and the cost to make one sale has jumped from $85 to $135. The instinct is to suspect the new Stripe checkout, but the data points the other way. Everything from the click downward (landing page, add to cart, checkout, purchase) is holding up or improving. The damage is all at the top of the funnel, where a brand new ad account has lost the buyer history Facebook spent months learning. On top of that, the campaign that quietly produced most of our sales was never rebuilt on the new account. That combination, not Stripe, explains the drop.

1. The 18 days at a glance

This one chart holds two different stories. Up to 4 June, on the old account, ROAS sits comfortably between 1.8 and 2.5. After we cut over on 5 June, it drops into a jumpy 0.8 to 1.6 band and, five days in, still is not climbing. That flat, choppy line is exactly what a fresh account looks like before Facebook has worked out who to show your ads to.

Oh-I-see: the line does not recover after the move. A healthy new account climbs as it learns. Ours is flat, which means it is still stuck in the reset.

2. Two changes, one chart: which one actually hurt?

This is the part worth slowing down on. Because Stripe went live before the account move, the few days in between (1 to 4 June: Stripe on, still the old account) let us separate the two. Each red bar below is the ROAS we lost at that step. The Stripe step is small. The account-move step is almost three times bigger.
Stripe, all good

It cost just 0.20 of ROAS, and even that was really because we doubled daily spend at the same time. More people who added to cart finished checking out. Stripe helped.

The account move, the real hit

It cost 0.57 of ROAS while we were spending less than before. Cheaper traffic should help, not hurt, so the cause is the fresh-account reset, not the budget.

Oh-I-see: if Stripe were the villain, the drop would have shown up on 1 June. It did not. It showed up on 6 June, the day after the account move.

3. So where exactly is it leaking?

Think of the funnel as six steps, measured against the same 100,000 ad views in each period so the comparison is fair. Reading down each bar, you can see precisely where people fall out. The gap opens right at the top, at the click, and the steps below it stay healthy.
A: before Stripe B: Stripe, old account C: after the move
The leak: fewer clicks

Out of every 100,000 views, we used to get 1,334 clicks. Now we get 906. That is roughly 430 fewer clicks for the same reach, purely because the new account is targeting worse.

What is fine: the buying

Of the people who do reach the landing page, the same share still buy: 5.4% before, 5.5% now. The page, the offer and the new Stripe checkout are all doing their job.

One number looks alarming but is not: "added to cart" fell from 277 to 130 per 100k. That is a tracking shift, the cart step now lives inside Stripe, not a real drop. The proof is that "started checkout" actually went up (49 to 75) and purchases held. People are still buying, the cart event just fires differently now.
Oh-I-see: nothing is broken on the website. We are simply buying fewer, lower-quality clicks at the top, and everything downstream is fine.

6. The one that matters most: our best campaign is missing

Our Cost-Cap scaling campaign (running since 30 January) was the engine. It held a ROAS of 1.9 to 2.3 every single day right up to 4 June and produced 476 sales across the window. We switched it off on 5 June and never rebuilt it on the new account. Instead, the new budget is piling into a testing campaign led by one ad that is just treading water.

7. What it is costing us, and what is NOT to blame

$13–18k
This is roughly the revenue we missed over these 5 days. If the same $23.5k had held its old efficiency instead of dropping to 1.27 ROAS, we would have earned this much more. That is about $2.7k to $3.6k a day, or on the order of $80k to $100k a month if the new account stays where it is.
Things we can stop worrying about
  • It is not Stripe or the checkout. More people finished checkout after Stripe, not fewer. The "added to cart" number dropped, but only because that step now happens inside Stripe. The sales held up.
  • It is not ad fatigue. How often people see our ads barely moved (about 1.3 the whole time). The rising costs are the fresh-account reset, not people tiring of the ads.
  • It is not the landing page or the offer. The same share of people land, and average order value actually went up ($150 to $171). People who arrive still buy, and buy a little more.
Oh-I-see: the easy suspects are all innocent. The one real cause is the account reset plus the missing campaign.

8. What I would do about it

First, todayRebuild the Cost-Cap scaling campaign on FluffCoRecreate the 30 January setup with the proven winners (DECW1_51A, MARW2_20A, the APRW5 ads). This is the single biggest lever, and it is money we can recover right now, not in three weeks.
NextLet the account settle, stop poking itIt is only 5 days old and still learning. Pull budget into fewer, well-fed ad sets so each one gets enough sales to exit learning, and resist daily edits. Every change restarts the clock.
AlsoDouble-check the pixel and the Stripe purchase signalRising costs plus fewer clicks can also mean Facebook is flying half-blind. Make sure purchases (including the Stripe-hosted ones) fire with their dollar value and land on the new pixel. Checkout looks healthy, so this is likely fine, but worth confirming the value is flowing.
ThenDo not scale until ROAS holds above 1.6 for a few days, and scale the proven campaign, not the testerWe are at 1.27 now, well under the old 1.84 to 2.04. Once the rebuilt Cost-Cap campaign clears target for 3 days straight, that is the one to pour budget into.

TL;DR · the whole story in four lines

  • Stripe is not the problem. Checkout actually got better after it went live on 1 June. More people who add to cart now finish buying.
  • The account move is the problem. Starting fresh on FluffCo reset everything Facebook had learned, so we now pay more to reach people who click less.
  • Our best campaign was not rebuilt. The Cost-Cap scaler that drove most sales got switched off on 5 June and never recreated. Half the new budget sits on one ad that barely breaks even.
  • It is fixable, and worth real money. Roughly $13k to $18k of revenue slipped in just 5 days. Rebuilding the proven campaign is the fastest way back.
One caveat to read it with: the new account only has 5 days of data, so treat the dollar figures as direction, not precision. The structural facts (missing campaign, 319 ads down to 17, half the budget on one ad) come straight from the ad-level export and are solid. We have not yet confirmed whether the Cost-Cap campaign was paused on purpose or simply never recreated. We can check that live in Meta before acting.
